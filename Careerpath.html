How to Become a Lab Analyst

Lab technicians often have a mix of education, training, skills and certifications to perform their job effectively.

Education
The type of training a laboratory analyst receives depends on his or her area of ​​expertise.
Educational institutions often instruct inspection technicians about specific inspection procedures during on-the-job training.
Training may include learning specific methods, generating reports, and properly disposing of waste.
Lab Techs can also receive off-the-job training in the form of science seminars and conferences.
There are various conferences where laboratory technicians can attend depending on their area of ​​expertise and area of ​​expertise.
During these meetings, they can learn more about their disciplines to keep up with industry trends and scientific discoveries.

Certifications
Medical Lab Technician certification:
MLT demonstrates the ability to work competently in medical laboratories.
To earn an MLT, you must have an associate degree and complete extensive training.
Once you meet the prerequisites, you need to take the exam.


Medical Laboratory Scientist certification:
MLS certification shows that you can take greater responsibility in the medical laboratory.
A bachelor's degree and laboratory experience is required to earn an MLS.
A further prerequisite is to obtain another relevant certification and complete the training program.
As with MLT certification, you must pass an exam.

Skills and Habits

Problem-solving:
The laboratory technician needs to be able to review various data and select the appropriate solution.
The problem-solving process must be systematic and thorough in order to achieve satisfactory results.

Analysis:
Laboratory technicians often need to collect and analyze large amounts of data and information.
They should be able to find the connection of results using analytical techniques.

Communication:
Oral communication skills are essential because lab technicians often work closely with colleagues.
They should be able to work together to complete tasks and experiments.
Additionally, lab techs should have excellent written communication skills as they need to write
reports on their findings so that others can understand the information.


How to Become a Food Scientist

To become a food scientist, you need to meet specific educational and training requirements.

Education
To be a food scientist, you would require at least a bachelor's degree, but most employers prefer either a master's or doctoral degree.
You should also complete science and engineering courses at both levels. Coursework includes food processing, food laws and regulations,
and food analysis. Emerging food scientists need training in nutrition, food safety, processing, and computer science.


Certifications
Although certifications won't always be a requirement to work as a food scientist, they can certainly help.
Certifications for food scientists can be obtained through the American Society of Agronomy and the American
Registry of Professional Animal Scientists. Most certifications require you to work in the field for a
period of time before taking the exam.


Skills and Habits

Technology:
Most food scientists use technology and computer programs to experiment and analyze data.
Working with computer programs and being able to code can be useful in this position.

Communication:
Requires good written and verbal communication skills.
Scientists contact team members to discuss projects and report results.

Research:
Most of the food scientist's position is research. Sufficient
research knowledge of food production and quality is important in this role.
