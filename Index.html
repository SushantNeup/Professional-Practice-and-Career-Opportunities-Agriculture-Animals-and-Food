Computers in the Food Industry


The food industry is responsible for supplying food to the United States and many of its trading partners.
To meet future demand, industry leaders have invested in robotics and automation technology to improve efficiency and scalability.
The most important areas of development include automatic irrigation, fertilization, harvesting and breeding systems.
Improvements in these processes are aimed at reducing production costs and saving water, fuel and fertilizer.


Many of these technologies are not only efficient, but also replace the
associated workforce. Partly spurred by the lack of human resources to harvest fruit, some large
trading companies have deployed harvesting robots that can cover the acreage of several workers.
Given the impact of COVID-19 on the US border and worker flow, many companies
have a strong incentive to continue investing in these technologies to further reduce their workforce needs.
With the advent of self-driving tractors and sprayers, the need for personnel is further reduced.
Further in the supply chain, automation of distribution warehouses and grocery stores is accelerating.
Many warehouses have replaced traditional forklifts with automatic guided
vehicles (AGVs).This allows you to perform many tasks that were previously performed by multiple workers.
Unloading and loading trucks, moving large luggage in the warehouse, etc.
In addition, it can be operated for a long time even under harsh conditions such as a freezer or a refrigerated environment.


According to McKinsey & Company's analysis, nearly three-quarters of hospitality and accommodation tasks can be automated. These include various tasks.
Industrial robots work with AI, thermal scanners, and lasers to chop vegetables, grill hamburgers and other foods, and perform similar tasks.
AI is currently being used to improve cooking processes, such as recipe optimization and ingredient selection.
Similarly, the front end of the restaurant is undergoing major technology-driven changes.
In particular, we have seen a shift to more automated services that allow cashiers to place orders, make payments and assign tables using voice or facial recognition.


Computers in the Agriculture Industry

Today, agriculture is not limited to the production of crops, livestock and related activities.
The challenges posed by ecological factors affecting the environment should be a primary consideration for any type of agricultural activity.
Farmers need to anticipate the environmental impact of climate change and this is where modern technology comes to the rescue.
In a nutshell, the uses of computers in agriculture include:

There is software to help forecast weather conditions and estimate agricultural production.
Computers are used to keep records of information related to costs associated with production, transportation, agricultural processes and to estimate and calculate profits and/or losses.
The Internet facilitates communication between farmers and between farmers and agricultural professionals.
This leads to the exchange of knowledge and guidance for farmers to improve their production and realize profits.
Through the use of software technology, farming activities have evolved into those that require less effort and lead to larger yields.
Mechanization has reduced human/animal effort and increased production speed and quality.

Geographic Information Systems (GIS) were used to develop rating systems for soil assessment and provide site assessments to help with what is now known as precision farming.
These high-tech interactive systems provide information based on a variety of factors such as soil conditions, drainage conditions and slopes, soil pH and nutrient status, and more.
Prior to the use of these systems, farmers were often unaware of soil production and unpredictable weather conditions affecting crop quality and profitability.
Precision farming gives farmers control by predicting key information including fertilization and drainage problems, insects and weeds.

Automated farm equipment, needless to say, ranks against human-driven equipment in terms of consistency and reliability.
Engine and machine functions such as transmission and hydraulic power are controlled using microchips integrated in the device.
Guide technology has been widely used in self-propelled devices to facilitate seeding and fertilizing crops.
Automated feeding systems, computerized milking and collecting machines have been around since the late 20th century, bringing greater economic benefits to the dairy industry as well as livestock.
Automatic transmission tractors are probably not far away.

Computers in the Animal Industry

When it comes to livestock production computer applications are available to monitor animals, store and evaluate information such as age, health records, milk production, offspring yield and the state of the reproductive cycle.
This is commonly known as swarm registration. Likewise, most farm accounting software and other agricultural computer applications provide record keeping, simulation of forecast-based models using this data, and estimation of income and productivity.
Most farm software vendors give you the flexibility to customize their applications to meet the specific needs of your farm or ranch. At the current stage of development of agribusinesses, one of the conditions for them to function well is information support, which includes the collection and processing of information necessary to make management decisions. Automated process control systems in livestock complexes accomplish up to 80% of all emerging management planning tasks.

Animal genomics can be defined as the study of looking at the entire gene landscape of a living animal and how they interact with each other to influence the animal’s growth and development.
Genomics help livestock producers understand the genetic risk of their herds and determine the future profitability of their livestock.
By being strategic with animal selection and breeding decisions, cattle genomics allows producers to optimize the profitability and yields of livestock herds.
